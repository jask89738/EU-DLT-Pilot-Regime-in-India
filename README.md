# EU-DLT-Pilot-Regime-in-India
This microsite presents Coinbase's position paper on the EU DLT Pilot Regime, reframed as a "Live Project for India" to explore how India can learn from Europe's regulatory experimentation with distributed ledger technology in capital markets.

The EU DLT Pilot Regime, launched in 2023, was designed to allow market participants to test tokenised financial instruments under a temporary regulatory sandbox. While groundbreaking in concept, Coinbase argues that restrictive caps, narrow participant eligibility, and limited instrument scope have held back its potential.

This project distils Coinbase's 10 key recommendations—ranging from fast-tracking regime revisions to embracing DeFi settlement models—and contextualises them for India's rapidly evolving digital public infrastructure landscape.

Policy Framework
10 Recommendations

1
Fast-track the revised EU DLT pilot regime
The current timeline risks losing momentum. An accelerated revision by 2026–27 would give market participants the certainty needed to commit capital and engineering resources to tokenised securities.

Recommendation:

The Commission should fast-track the revised DLT Pilot Regime to provide regulatory clarity and encourage infrastructure investment before the competitive window closes.

2
Remove the market capitalisation cap
The €6 billion cap on total market value of DLT-transferable securities severely limits participation. Institutional-grade products require access to larger, more liquid pools.

Recommendation:

Remove the €6 billion aggregate market capitalisation threshold and any per-issuer limits to enable meaningful institutional adoption of DLT-based markets.

3
Allow CASPs to operate DLT market infrastructures
Crypto-Asset Service Providers already have deep technical expertise with distributed ledger technology. Excluding them from operating MTFs or settlement systems creates an unnecessary bottleneck.

Recommendation:

Permit authorised CASPs to operate DLT Trading and Settlement Systems (DLT TSS), leveraging their existing technological and operational capabilities.

4
Enable tokenised cash for settlement
Settlement currently depends on traditional cash legs, undermining the efficiency gains of on-chain trading. Tokenised forms of money—stablecoins, tokenised deposits, and CBDC—can enable atomic, real-time settlement.

Recommendation:

Recognise regulated stablecoins, tokenised commercial bank deposits, and wholesale CBDC as valid settlement assets within the DLT Pilot Regime.

5
Adopt technology-neutral DeFi settlement models
Permissionless blockchain infrastructure and smart-contract-based settlement can reduce counterparty risk and settlement times. The regime should not prescribe technology choices.

Recommendation:

Adopt a technology-neutral stance that accommodates both permissioned and permissionless DLT, including smart-contract-based (DeFi) settlement models.

6
Extend the scope to all MiFID financial instruments
Limiting the pilot to bonds and equities ignores the broader opportunity. Derivatives, structured products, and fund units could all benefit from tokenisation.

Recommendation:

Expand the scope of eligible instruments under the DLT Pilot Regime to cover all financial instruments defined under MiFID II.

7
Streamline the exemption framework
The current process for obtaining exemptions from existing MiFID/CSDR requirements is lengthy and uncertain, deterring potential participants.

Recommendation:

Simplify and standardise the exemption process, providing clear criteria and timelines for regulatory decisions on exemption requests.

8
Facilitate cross-border interoperability
Fragmented national implementations undermine the single-market objective. Standardised protocols and mutual recognition are essential for pan-European DLT markets.

Recommendation:

Mandate interoperability standards and mutual recognition of DLT market infrastructures across EU Member States.

9
Clarify the transition from pilot to permanent regime
Without a clear path from pilot to full regime, firms face stranded investment risk. A defined transition mechanism is critical for long-term planning.

Recommendation:

Establish a transparent transition pathway from the pilot regime to a permanent regulatory framework, including grandfathering provisions for pilot participants.

10
Engage global partners including India
Capital markets are global. The EU should seek alignment with other jurisdictions developing DLT frameworks to avoid regulatory arbitrage and foster cross-border investment.

Recommendation:

Proactively engage with global partners—including India, the UK, Singapore, and the US—to align DLT regulatory frameworks and support cross-border tokenised securities markets.

The EU DLT Pilot Regime represents a landmark experiment in bringing traditional capital markets onchain. While its ambition is commendable, the current framework's limitations risk squandering Europe's first-mover advantage. Bold, timely reform can position the EU—and its global partners—at the forefront of the tokenised economy.

🇮🇳 Why this matters for India
As India builds its own digital public infrastructure and capital market frameworks, the EU's experience with the DLT Pilot Regime offers key lessons: avoid restrictive caps, enable CASPs, and embrace DeFi models to leapfrog into the onchain future.
